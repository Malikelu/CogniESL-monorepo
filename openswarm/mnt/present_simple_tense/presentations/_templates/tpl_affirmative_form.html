03 // Present Simple

Affirmative Form

Present simple affirmative sentences follow a clear subject + verb structure. The verb form changes with the subject, so agreement is the key grammar decision in every sentence.

Core Pattern

General form: Subject + verb + object/complement.

Third-person singular: He/She/It + verb with -s or -es + object/complement.

Purpose: Use this form for routines, habits, and facts.

I / You / We / They

Base Verb Stays Unchanged

With these subjects, the verb remains in its base form in affirmative present simple sentences. This pattern keeps statements direct and easy to build.

Examples include “I work early” and “They study at the library,” where no extra ending is added to the verb.

Common Everyday Verbs

High-frequency verbs like work, live, read, play, cook, and study are often used with these subjects to describe daily routines. They are ideal for speaking and writing practice.

You can create useful patterns such as “We read every morning” and “You cook dinner at home” to reinforce fluency.

He / She / It

Add -s in Most Cases

When the subject is he, she, or it, most affirmative verbs take an -s ending to show correct agreement. This is the default third-person singular rule.

For example, “He walks to work” and “She reads before bed” both follow the standard -s pattern.

Add -es After Specific Endings

Verbs ending in s, sh, ch, x, or o usually add -es in third-person singular forms. This spelling rule supports natural pronunciation in connected speech.

A clear model is “She watches TV,” and another common form is “He goes to school” in daily conversation.

Quick Conversion Practice

Take one base-form sentence and switch the subject to third-person singular, then update the verb ending. This contrast helps learners spot agreement changes quickly and accurately.

Example pair: “They walk to work” becomes “He walks to work,” and “They watch TV” becomes “She watches TV.”
