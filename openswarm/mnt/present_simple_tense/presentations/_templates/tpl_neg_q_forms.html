Negative and Question Forms

Affirmative present simple uses the main verb directly, but negatives and questions need the auxiliary do/does. The helper carries agreement, so the main verb returns to base form in both structures.

Affirmative Baseline Before Transformation

Start from this pattern: I/You/We/They + base verb and He/She/It + verb-s. When you add do or does, the main verb drops any third-person -s.

She likes coffee. becomes She does not like coffee. and Does she like coffee?

DO → I / You / We / They

DOES → He / She / It

Negative Form

Subject + Do/Does + Not + Base Verb

Use do not or does not before the verb to show a habit or fact that is not true. In conversation, don’t and doesn’t are common and equally grammatical.

With he/she/it, choose does not and keep the main verb plain, not -s form. This avoids errors such as doesn’t likes.

I do not like coffee.

She does not like coffee.

They do not work on Sundays.

He doesn’t play tennis after class.

Question Form

Do/Does + Subject + Base Verb?

For yes/no questions, place Do or Does before the subject and then use the base verb. This inversion is the clearest contrast with affirmative order.

Short answers repeat the same auxiliary, so form stays consistent across question and response. Example: Does she like coffee? → Yes, she does. / No, she doesn’t.

Do you like coffee?

Does he like coffee?

Do they live near the station?

Does it open at nine?
